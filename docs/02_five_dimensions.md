# 02 — Five Policy-Grounded Dimensions

HEART derives its **five benchmarkable ethical dimensions** from a fixed set
of public policy and governance documents, plus internal technology-ethics
files. The dimensions are *not* invented by us, and they are *not* taken from
benchmark names. They are the dimensions that recur across the policy
sources below.

## Policy source families

| Source family | Documents used |
|---|---|
| International AI ethics principles | UNESCO Recommendation on the Ethics of AI (2021); OECD AI Principles (2019) |
| Risk management & governance | NIST AI Risk Management Framework (2023); EU AI Act (2024) |
| Data protection law | GDPR (2016); PIPL (2021) |
| Internal technology ethics files | Internal human-centeredness, inclusive integrity, safety and reliability, privacy protection, trustworthiness and controllability, and open co-governance requirements |

## The mapping

The first five requirements below are **benchmarkable behavioural
dimensions** — they describe something a model can do or fail to do in a test.
The sixth (open co-governance) is a **cross-cutting governance requirement**
that the rubrics treat as a process condition rather than a model behaviour.

| Dimension | Policy support |
|---|---|
| **Human-Centered** | Human dignity, autonomy, well-being, human oversight, and human agency in UNESCO / OECD / EU AI Act / NIST AI RMF |
| **Fairness & Inclusiveness** | Fairness, non-discrimination, accessibility, vulnerable-group protection, and inclusion requirements across UNESCO / OECD / EU AI Act |
| **Safety & Reliability** | Safety, robustness, misuse prevention, risk management, and reliable operation requirements in NIST AI RMF / EU AI Act |
| **Trustworthiness & Controllability** | Transparency, explainability, accountability, auditability, controllability, and oversight in UNESCO / OECD / NIST AI RMF / EU AI Act |
| **Privacy Protection** | Data minimisation, consent, contextual processing, data-subject rights, confidentiality, and personal-information protection in GDPR / PIPL / internal files |

## Benchmarkable scope per dimension

The mapping is operationalised in the workbook by tagging each of the 103
reviewed papers with one primary dimension. The benchmarkable scope for each
dimension is:

| Dimension | What the benchmark must produce evidence for |
|---|---|
| **Human-Centered** | Moral reasoning, empathy, well-being, professional boundaries, human agency |
| **Fairness & Inclusiveness** | Stereotyping, disparate treatment, representational harm, cultural coverage, affected-community evidence |
| **Safety & Reliability** | Dangerous assistance, jailbreak resistance, over-refusal, misuse, recovery after unsafe behaviour |
| **Trustworthiness & Controllability** | Factuality, evidence support, sycophancy, temporal validity, rule-constrained reasoning, human control |
| **Privacy Protection** | Memorisation, PII leakage, inferential disclosure, confidentiality, legal compliance, contextual integrity |

## How the mapping is used

The 14 rubrics in [`03_fourteen_rubrics.md`](03_fourteen_rubrics.md) are
identical across dimensions — but the **anchors and red lines** are
dimension-specific. A "normative source ledger" for Fairness draws on
anti-discrimination law and community evidence; the same rubric for Privacy
draws on GDPR/PIPL and contextual integrity literature.

## Why these five and not more

We piloted with seven dimensions (adding "explainability" and "robustness"
as separate axes). The seventh and eighth collapsed back into Trustworthiness
& Controllability and Safety & Reliability respectively because the rubrics
and the source papers we audit cannot distinguish them at the construct
level. We kept the merged form to avoid the leaderboard pathology of
inventing axes that don't correspond to distinct claims.
